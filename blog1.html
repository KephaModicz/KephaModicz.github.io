Phishing & Social Engineering: How Attackers Trick You

By Kepha Dickson • Oct 3, 2025 • Security & Awareness

Phishing and social engineering are among the most common paths attackers use to gain access to accounts, credentials, or sensitive data. These attacks exploit human trust more than technical vulnerabilities — which makes awareness and careful habits crucial for defence.

What is phishing?

Phishing is a scam where attackers pose as a trusted source (bank, colleague, service provider) to trick victims into revealing information, clicking malicious links, or opening infected attachments.

Common phishing techniques

  • Email phishing: Fake emails asking you to reset passwords or confirm payments.
  • Spear phishing: Targeted, personalised messages aimed at specific individuals or roles.
  • Smishing & vishing: SMS (smishing) or voice-call (vishing) scams that request sensitive info.
  • Malicious attachments & links: Files or URLs that install malware or direct to credential-harvesting pages.

What is social engineering?

Social engineering covers a broader set of manipulative techniques — including phishing — where attackers influence people to break normal security procedures. Tactics include impersonation, pretexting (making up stories), baiting, and elicitation (casual questioning).

Real-world examples

  • CEO fraud: An attacker poses as the CEO and requests an urgent wire transfer from finance.
  • Account recovery scam: An SMS claims your account is locked and asks you to verify via a malicious link.
  • Public Wi-Fi baiting: Fake Wi-Fi hotspots that capture login credentials.

How to spot phishing & social engineering

  • Unexpected urgency or pressure to act now.
  • Requests for sensitive info (passwords, 2FA codes) via email or phone.
  • Sender address or domain that looks similar but slightly off (typosquatting).
  • Generic greetings (e.g., "Dear user") instead of your name — especially in targeted contexts.
  • Links that don’t match the visible text — hover to check before clicking.

Practical prevention steps

Enable MFA

Use multi-factor authentication for all important accounts to add an extra layer of security.

Verify requests

Confirm unusual requests through a different channel (call a known number) before acting.

Keep software updated

Apply OS and application updates — they close vulnerabilities attackers exploit.

Use phishing-resistant methods

Deploy hardware tokens or passkeys where possible and avoid password reuse.

What to do if you’re targeted

  • Don’t click or reply. If you clicked, disconnect from the network and run antivirus scans.
  • Change passwords immediately for any accounts that might be compromised (from a safe device).
  • Report the incident to your IT/security team or the platform involved.
  • Consider enabling fraud monitoring for financial accounts affected.

Closing thoughts

Attackers rely on human error and urgency. Strong technical controls help, but the most reliable defence is a combination of security-aware people, up-to-date systems, and clear reporting procedures.
